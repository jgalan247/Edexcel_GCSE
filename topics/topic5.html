Emerging technologies, ethical considerations, legal frameworks, and environmental issues

5A: Emerging Technologies

Key Definition

Emerging technologies are new technologies that are currently developing or will be developed in the near future. They have the potential to significantly impact society, business, and everyday life.

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Key Definition

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to computer systems designed to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence, such as learning, problem-solving, pattern recognition, and decision-making.

Types of AI

Machine Learning

AI that learns from data to improve its performance without being explicitly programmed. Used in recommendation systems, spam filters, and fraud detection.

Neural Networks

Systems modelled on the human brain, using interconnected nodes (neurons) to process information. Used in image and speech recognition.

Natural Language Processing

AI that understands and generates human language. Powers virtual assistants like Siri and Alexa, chatbots, and translation services.

Applications of AI

  • Healthcare: Diagnosing diseases, analysing medical images, drug discovery
  • Transport: Self-driving cars, traffic management, route optimisation
  • Finance: Fraud detection, algorithmic trading, credit scoring
  • Customer Service: Chatbots, virtual assistants, personalised recommendations
  • Manufacturing: Quality control, predictive maintenance, robotics

Autonomous Vehicles

Key Definition

Autonomous vehicles are vehicles capable of sensing their environment and navigating without human input. They use sensors, cameras, radar, and AI to make driving decisions.

Benefits Concerns
Reduced human error accidents Job losses for drivers (taxi, lorry, bus)
Mobility for elderly/disabled Ethical decisions (who to protect in crash?)
Improved traffic flow Security risks (hacking)
More efficient fuel use Liability issues if accidents occur

Internet of Things (IoT)

Key Definition

The Internet of Things (IoT) refers to the network of physical devices embedded with sensors, software, and connectivity that enables them to collect and exchange data over the internet.

Examples of IoT Devices

Smart Home

Thermostats, lighting, security cameras, doorbells, appliances

Wearables

Fitness trackers, smartwatches, health monitors

Industry

Sensors in machinery, supply chain tracking, automation

Smart Cities

Traffic management, waste collection, street lighting

IoT Security Concerns

  • Many IoT devices have weak security
  • Devices can be hacked and added to botnets
  • Privacy concerns - devices collect personal data
  • Difficult to update/patch vulnerabilities

Biometrics

Key Definition

Biometrics is the measurement and analysis of unique physical or behavioural characteristics for identification and authentication purposes.

Type Examples
Physiological Fingerprints, facial recognition, iris/retina scans, DNA
Behavioural Voice recognition, typing patterns, gait analysis, signature

Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Cannot be forgotten like passwords
  • Difficult to forge or steal
  • Convenient and quick to use
  • Unique to each individual

Disadvantages

  • Privacy concerns (stored biometric data)
  • If compromised, cannot be changed
  • Can be fooled (photos, recordings)
  • Expensive to implement

5B: Ethical, Legal & Environmental Issues

Ethical Issues

Key Definition

Ethics in computing refers to the moral principles that govern how technology should be developed and used. It involves considering what is right and wrong in the use of computers and data.

Key Ethical Concerns

Privacy

Right to control personal information. Concerns about surveillance, data collection, and tracking of online activities.

Digital Divide

Gap between those with access to technology and those without. Creates inequality in education, employment, and services.

Automation & Jobs

AI and robots replacing human workers. Need for retraining, concerns about unemployment and income inequality.

AI Bias

AI systems can inherit biases from training data, leading to unfair decisions in hiring, lending, or criminal justice.

Fake News

Spread of misinformation online. Difficulty distinguishing real from fake content. Impact on democracy and public opinion.

Cyberbullying

Online harassment and bullying. Anonymity enables harmful behaviour. Mental health impacts on victims.

Legal Issues and Legislation

Key Definition

Legislation refers to laws passed by government that regulate how technology and data can be used. Breaking these laws can result in fines or imprisonment.

Key UK Legislation

Data Protection Act 2018 (UK GDPR)

Controls how personal data is collected, stored, and used by organisations.

Key Principles:

  • Data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently
  • Collected for specific, legitimate purposes only
  • Limited to what is necessary
  • Kept accurate and up to date
  • Not kept longer than necessary
  • Processed securely

Rights: Access your data, have errors corrected, request deletion, object to processing

Computer Misuse Act 1990

Makes it illegal to access or modify computer systems without authorisation.

Three Main Offences:

  • Section 1: Unauthorised access to computer material (hacking)
  • Section 2: Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences
  • Section 3: Unauthorised modification of computer material (e.g., spreading viruses)

Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

Protects the intellectual property of creators.

What it protects:

  • Software and source code
  • Music, films, and images
  • Written content and documents
  • Database designs

Illegal: Copying, distributing, or modifying without permission. Includes software piracy and illegal downloads.

Software Licensing

Defines how software can legally be used.

Type Description
Proprietary Paid software with restrictions. Cannot modify or redistribute.
Open Source Free to use, modify, and distribute. Source code available.
Freeware Free to use but cannot modify. Source code not available.
Shareware Free trial with limited features. Pay for full version.

Environmental Issues

Key Definition

E-waste (electronic waste) refers to discarded electrical or electronic devices. It is one of the fastest growing waste streams globally and contains hazardous materials.

Environmental Concerns

Energy Consumption

  • Data centres use enormous amounts of electricity
  • Cryptocurrency mining is energy-intensive
  • Devices on standby still consume power
  • Manufacturing requires significant energy

E-Waste Problems

  • Toxic materials (lead, mercury, cadmium)
  • Short device lifespans (planned obsolescence)
  • Difficult to recycle complex electronics
  • Often shipped to developing countries

Reducing Environmental Impact

Strategy Description
Reduce Keep devices longer, avoid unnecessary upgrades, use energy-efficient settings
Reuse Donate or sell old devices, buy refurbished equipment
Recycle Use proper e-waste recycling centres, reclaim valuable materials
Renewable Energy Power data centres with solar, wind, or hydroelectric power
Efficient Design Create devices that are easier to repair and recycle, longer-lasting

June 2023 - Paper 1 - Q17 4 marks

Explain two ways in which the Data Protection Act 2018 protects individuals. [4 marks]

Show Mark Scheme Answer

Any two protections with explanation (2 marks each):

  • Right to access: Individuals can request a copy of all personal data held about them, allowing them to see what information organisations have
  • Right to rectification: Individuals can request that inaccurate data is corrected, ensuring records are truthful
  • Right to erasure: Individuals can request their data is deleted when no longer needed, giving them control over their information
  • Data must be kept secure: Organisations must protect data from breaches, preventing identity theft and fraud
  • Data minimisation: Only necessary data should be collected, limiting exposure of personal information
June 2022 - Paper 1 - Q18 6 marks

(a) State two offences under the Computer Misuse Act 1990. [2 marks]

(b) Describe two ways computer technology can have a negative impact on the environment. [4 marks]

Show Mark Scheme Answer

(a) Any two of:

  • Unauthorised access to computer material (1 mark)
  • Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences (1 mark)
  • Unauthorised modification of computer material (1 mark)

(b) Any two with description:

  • E-waste: Discarded devices contain toxic materials that can pollute soil and water if not disposed of properly (2 marks)
  • Energy consumption: Data centres and devices use large amounts of electricity, often from fossil fuels, contributing to carbon emissions (2 marks)
  • Manufacturing: Production of devices uses resources and energy, creates pollution and greenhouse gases (2 marks)
  • Short lifespans: Planned obsolescence means devices are replaced frequently, increasing waste (2 marks)
